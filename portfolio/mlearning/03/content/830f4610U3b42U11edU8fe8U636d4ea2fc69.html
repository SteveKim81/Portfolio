Adaptive vs responsive design

Adaptive Design

Crossing the Boundary

​

​

Adaptive and responsive are the same in that they change appearance based on the browser environment they are viewing.

Adaptive

Adaptive websites adapt to the width of the browser at specific points. In other words, the website is only concerned about the browser being a specific width, at which point it adapts the layout.

Pros

The most significant benefit of adaptive design? A tailor-made approach. Instead of scaling a desktop website up or down to meet user device demands, adaptive initiatives let developers create the best-fit experience for unique device viewpoints. For example, smaller-width designs can be made simpler and streamlined to account for reduced screen size. At the same time, higher-resolution offerings can include more content and greater control to deliver ideal UX.

​

Cons

The best-fit nature of the adaptive design makes it less flexible than responsive design alternatives. Developers must create multiple design templates based on standard widths, but users may still encounter form or function issues if their screen size slightly exceeds the norm. Time is also a concern — companies must have the available staff resources or be willing to spend on outside help to identify key GUI elements, design multiple instances, and deploy them at scale.

RESPONSIVE

Responsive websites respond to the size of the browser at any given point. No matter the browser width, the site adjusts its layout (and perhaps functionality) to optimize the screen.

Pros

When it comes to responsive design, the cost is a critical factor. Instead of spending on development, testing, and iteration for six separate site templates, companies can design a single version that automatically scales up or down on demand.

​

​

Cons

While responsive design should deliver a tailored experience for users no matter their device size, issues with CSS or HTML markers could cause problems in responsive screen sizing. In addition, outside elements such as advertisements may load poorly, and visual elements are at risk of shifting on-screen in response to differing screen sizes. It’s also worth noting that many responsive designs are scaled versions of desktop sites, meaning they can take far longer to load on mobile devices than best-fit adaptive offerings.

Another way to think about it is the difference between smooth and snap design. Responsive design is smooth because the layout fluidly adjusts regardless of the device.
